\begin{abstract}
\noindent Cohen's kappa ($\kappa$), the prevalence-adjusted bias-adjusted kappa (PABAK), and Gwet's Agreement Coefficient (AC1) are commonly used statistics for measuring inter-rater agreement. When applied to binary ratings from two raters under conditional independence, they quantify agreement beyond what chance alone would produce. However, the three metrics define ``chance'' differently. Applied to the same data, they can produce conflicting conclusions about the quality of agreement between the same raters. A $\kappa$ of 0.20 at low prevalence and 0.60 at balanced prevalence can reflect identical diagnostic accuracy. Through Monte Carlo simulation (24.1 million replications, 4,370 scenarios across 38 rater profiles and 23 prevalence levels), we show that traditional qualitative labels \citep{landis1977measurement} are prevalence-dependent: $\kappa$-based labels correctly classify rater quality in 100\% of scenarios at balanced prevalence (1,330 of 1,330) but only 36.1\% at extreme prevalence (412 of 1,140). No single metric with fixed labels achieves reliable classification across the entire prevalence spectrum. To address this, we develop GRASS (Guide for Rater Agreement under Structural Skew), a structured protocol for interpreting existing agreement metrics that conditions on prevalence to reliably assess rater quality regardless of base rate. GRASS consists of a four-step flowchart, a prevalence-indexed threshold table for $\kappa$, PABAK, and AC1, and a minimum reporting checklist.
\end{abstract}

%% ============================================================
\section*{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

Inter-rater agreement studies require a defensible measure of whether raters agree beyond chance. Cohen's kappa ($\kappa$), the prevalence-adjusted bias-adjusted kappa (PABAK), and Gwet's Agreement Coefficient (AC1) are the most widely used statistics for this purpose. All three quantify agreement beyond what chance alone would produce, but each defines ``chance'' differently: $\kappa$ estimates chance agreement from the raters' observed marginal distributions, PABAK assumes a fixed 50\% chance agreement regardless of the data, and AC1 adapts its chance estimate to the overall distribution of ratings. The behavior of each metric depends on prevalence, the base rate of positive cases in the data being rated. When prevalence is extreme, these different chance models can produce conflicting conclusions from identical data. Practitioners typically interpret these metrics using fixed qualitative labels---``slight,'' ``moderate,'' ``substantial''---introduced by \citet{landis1977measurement}. A $\kappa$ of 0.20 at prevalence 0.01 can reflect the same underlying rater accuracy as a $\kappa$ of 0.60 at prevalence 0.50, yet the first receives a ``slight'' label and the second a ``moderate'' one. The labels suggest a change in rater quality where none occurred. \citet{feinstein1990high} described this behavior as a paradox of the kappa statistic.

The critique is not new, however. \citet{feinstein1990high} and \citet{cicchetti1990high} identified the problem over three decades ago, and subsequent work has consistently challenged the use of fixed labels for chance-corrected agreement. \citet{hoehler2000bias} proposed a prevalence-invariant alternative to $\kappa$, and others have critiqued fixed labels from different angles \citep{gwet2002kappa, sim2005kappa, vanacore2022robustness, quarfoot2016robust}. Yet the Landis-Koch labels remain the default interpretation framework, applied not only to $\kappa$ but to metrics they were never designed for, including PABAK and AC1. No practical guide tells a researcher how to interpret a specific coefficient at a specific prevalence level. This study develops GRASS (Guide for Rater Agreement under Structural Skew) to fill that gap.

\section*{The GRASS Framework}
%% ============================================================

GRASS provides a structured decision procedure for interpreting binary inter-rater agreement that conditions interpretation on the study's operating conditions. The protocol produces one of three terminal outcomes: raters below threshold, minority-class weakness detected, or agreement adequate across both classes. Figure~\ref{fig:flowchart} summarizes the procedure and the steps are detailed below. The procedure begins by computing the full set of agreement metrics, establishes the prevalence context, compares metrics to prevalence-conditioned thresholds, and checks minority-class agreement.

The three metrics differ in how they estimate the agreement expected by chance. $\kappa$ derives its chance estimate from the raters' marginal distributions \citep{cohen1960coefficient}; PABAK fixes chance agreement at 0.50 and is a linear rescaling of observed agreement \citep{byrt1993bias}; AC1 adapts its chance term to the overall category distribution \citep{gwet2008computing}. Full formulas are given in Appendix~\ref{app:algebra}.

\subsection*{Step 1: Compute the full metric panel}

From the observed ratings, construct the $2 \times 2$ contingency table:

\begin{table}[h!]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc||c}
\toprule
 & R2 Negative & R2 Positive & Total \\
\midrule
R1 Negative & $a$ & $b$ & $a + b$ \\
R1 Positive & $c$ & $d$ & $c + d$ \\
\midrule\midrule
Total & $a + c$ & $b + d$ & $N$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent where $a$ and $d$ represent negative and positive agreement, respectively, and $b$ and $c$ represent disagreement. From this table, compute observed agreement ($P_0$), Cohen's $\kappa$, PABAK, Gwet's AC1, positive agreement ($P_{\text{pos}}$), negative agreement ($P_{\text{neg}}$), and the prevalence and bias indices (PI, BI). The prevalence index (\mbox{$\text{PI} = |d - a|/N$}) and bias index (\mbox{$\text{BI} = |b - c|/N$}) quantify marginal imbalance and rater bias in the table \citep{byrt1993bias}. Full definitions and formulas for all metrics are given in Appendix~\ref{app:algebra}. When $N < 50$ or prevalence is extreme (below 0.10 or above 0.90), use the logit-transformed confidence interval for $\kappa$ rather than the standard Wald interval. The Wald interval violates $\kappa$'s $[-1,1]$ parameter bounds in 9.4\% of replications at small $N$; the logit alternative reduces this to 1.4\% (Appendix~\ref{app:ci}). When $N < 30$, use a non-parametric bootstrap (resample subjects with replacement, recompute $\kappa$ for each resample, and take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles). Implementation is available in the accompanying code repository.

\subsection*{Step 2: Establish prevalence}

The interpretation of all three metrics depends on prevalence. Before consulting the threshold table in Step 3, establish the prevalence of the condition in your study. If prevalence is known from the study design or an external source, use it directly. If it must be estimated from the data, Appendix~\ref{app:prevalence} describes the estimation procedure and its assumptions. The $\kappa$ threshold changes slowly in the interior of the prevalence range but rapidly at the extremes. Prevalence estimates below 0.10 or above 0.90 should be accompanied by explicit uncertainty quantification.

\subsection*{Step 3: Look up prevalence-conditioned thresholds}

Using the prevalence established in Step 2, look up the corresponding thresholds for $\kappa$, PABAK, and AC1 in Table~\ref{tab:thresholds}. For prevalence values between tabulated rows, linear interpolation is appropriate. The table provides thresholds for both High and Medium quality; use the High threshold for safety-critical or clinical applications and the Medium threshold for exploratory or pilot studies. Compare your computed metrics to these values. If any metric falls below its threshold, the raters should be retrained, recalibrated, or the study redesigned before proceeding. When the three metrics disagree on whether the threshold is met, the discordance signals outsized marginal imbalance. Report all three values and note the discordance.

\subsection*{Step 4: Check minority-class agreement}

If all three metrics exceed their thresholds in Step 3, check agreement on the less frequent category. At low prevalence, positive cases are rare and the relevant measure is positive agreement ($P_{\text{pos}}$); at high prevalence, negative cases are rare and the relevant measure is negative agreement ($P_{\text{neg}}$). Check whether agreement on the less frequent category exceeds 0.50. This threshold marks the point at which raters agree on the minority class more often than they disagree---below 0.50, disagreements outnumber agreements for that class. In high-stakes applications, a stricter threshold (e.g., 0.70) may be appropriate.

At very low prevalence, this check will flag virtually all raters, including high-quality ones. This is not diagnosing poor rater quality; it is diagnosing insufficient positive-case volume. When fewer than 10 positive cases are expected ($N \times \text{prevalence} < 10$), $P_{\text{pos}}$ is too unstable to interpret, consistent with the minimum expected frequency condition for stable proportion estimates \citep{cochran1952chisquare, agresti2002categorical}. The appropriate response is to increase the sample size or enrich the sample for positive cases.

\subsection*{GRASS minimum reporting standard}

Agreement studies adopting GRASS should report, at minimum:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The $2 \times 2$ contingency table (cell counts $a, b, c, d$)
\item The prevalence of the condition being rated
\item $P_0$, $\kappa$, PABAK, and AC1
\item Positive and negative agreement ($P_{\text{pos}}$, $P_{\text{neg}}$)
\item Prevalence index and bias index
\item 95\% confidence intervals for $\kappa$ (logit-transformed when $N < 50$ or prevalence is extreme)
\end{enumerate}

A single coefficient with a qualitative label is not sufficient for defensible interpretation.

\subsection*{Worked example}

Consider a study evaluating whether two radiologists agree on the presence of a rare finding in 200 chest films. The population prevalence is 5\%. The observed ratings produce the following contingency table:

\begin{table}[h!]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc||c}
\toprule
 & R2 Negative & R2 Positive & Total \\
\midrule
R1 Negative & 180 & 5 & 185 \\
R1 Positive & 7 & 8 & 15 \\
\midrule\midrule
Total & 187 & 13 & 200 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent \textbf{Step 1:} From this table, $P_0 = 0.94$, $\kappa = 0.54$, $\text{PABAK} = 0.88$, $\text{AC1} = 0.93$, $P_{\text{pos}} = 0.57$, $P_{\text{neg}} = 0.97$, $\text{PI} = 0.86$, $\text{BI} = 0.01$. \textbf{Step 2:} Prevalence is 0.05, known from the study design. If prevalence were unknown, Appendix~\ref{app:prevalence} provides the estimation procedure; for this example, the estimated prevalence would be 0.07 with a 95\% CI of [0.035, 0.105], and the conclusion would be unchanged. \textbf{Step 3:} At prevalence 0.05, the thresholds are $\kappa \geq 0.243$, $\text{PABAK} \geq 0.639$, and $\text{AC1} \geq 0.754$ (Table~\ref{tab:thresholds}). All three metrics exceed their thresholds. \textbf{Step 4:} $P_{\text{pos}} = 0.57 \geq 0.50$. The raters pass, but the margin is thin. Agreement on positive cases is modest even though overall agreement is high.

Without GRASS, Landis-Koch labels produce three different conclusions from the same data: $\kappa$ at 0.54 is ``moderate,'' PABAK at 0.88 is ``almost perfect,'' and AC1 at 0.93 is ``almost perfect.'' A reviewer reading only $\kappa$ might question whether the raters are adequate. The prevalence-conditioned threshold resolves this: at 5\% prevalence, $\kappa = 0.54$ exceeds the threshold and the raters are performing well for their operating conditions. The high PI (0.86) confirms that prevalence imbalance, not poor rater quality, drives the low $\kappa$.

\section*{Simulation and Findings}
%% ============================================================

To evaluate how well existing metrics and labels recover true rater quality, we conducted a Monte Carlo simulation spanning 24.1 million replications across 4,370 scenarios (23 prevalence levels $\times$ 5 sample sizes $\times$ 38 rater profiles). The profile grid spans symmetric, within-rater asymmetric, between-rater asymmetric, and opposite-bias configurations across three quality bands. Full design details are in Appendix~\ref{app:sim}. Each scenario has a known ground-truth quality classification based on the minimum sensitivity or specificity across both raters: High (minimum Se or Sp $\geq 0.90$; 1,265 scenarios), Medium ($\geq 0.70$; 1,725 scenarios), Low ($< 0.70$; 1,380 scenarios). In the resulting grid, 73.5\% of scenarios are prevalence-dominant ($\text{PI} > \text{BI}$), 22.8\% are bias-dominant ($\text{BI} > \text{PI}$), and 3.8\% are balanced. The question is whether applying a metric with a fixed or prevalence-conditioned label recovers that ground truth, and whether the answer depends on which regime a scenario falls in.

\subsection*{Problems with current practice}

\subsubsection*{Qualitative labels are prevalence-dependent}

When Landis-Koch labels are applied to $\kappa$, the same rater pair can receive different qualitative labels depending on prevalence. Figure~\ref{fig:three_metrics} illustrates this for raters with Se $=$ Sp $= 0.90$: $\kappa$ crosses the ``substantial'' threshold only within the 0.20--0.80 prevalence range, despite no change in the raters' diagnostic accuracy. The labels assigned to PABAK and AC1 also shift across prevalence, though to different degrees and in different directions. Fixed qualitative labels do not produce stable conclusions across the prevalence spectrum.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/fig_three_metrics.png}
\caption{$\kappa$, PABAK, and AC1 for Se $=$ Sp $= 0.90$ raters ($N = 100$) across 23 prevalence levels. $\kappa$ varies from 0.06 at extreme prevalence to 0.64 at balanced prevalence despite unchanged rater quality. PABAK and AC1 are more stable but diverge from $\kappa$ at the extremes.}
\label{fig:three_metrics}

\subsubsection*{The three metrics diverge at extreme prevalence}

At balanced prevalence, $\kappa$, PABAK, and AC1 produce similar values (mean 0.37, 0.38, 0.39 across profiles). At extreme prevalence ($p = 0.01$), the same raters produce mean $\kappa = 0.05$, PABAK $= 0.38$, AC1 $= 0.48$. The divergence is not random: $\kappa$ is suppressed toward zero at extreme prevalence because its marginal-based chance estimate grows large; AC1 inflates because its adaptive chance term shrinks; PABAK remains stable because it assumes fixed chance agreement of 0.50 regardless of prevalence. The magnitude of the divergence is governed by $\Delta = \text{PI}^2 - \text{BI}^2$ (Appendix~\ref{app:algebra}). Reporting a single metric without reference to prevalence can produce a misleading picture of rater agreement.

\subsubsection*{Standard confidence intervals for $\kappa$ are unreliable at the boundary}

The standard Wald CI for $\kappa$ violated the $[-1,1]$ parameter bounds in 9.4\% of replications. The logit-transformed alternative reduced this to 1.4\%. Neither method achieves nominal 95\% coverage at extreme prevalence, even at large $N$ (Table~\ref{tab:coverage}). At prevalence 0.01, logit coverage plateaus near 90\% regardless of sample size. The logit interval consistently outperforms Wald by 1--3 percentage points and is the least conservative analytic option available, but it should not be treated as adequate at extreme prevalence. Bootstrap CIs should be used when prevalence is extreme, and are essential below $N = 30$ where coverage can fall below 60\%.

\subsection*{How prevalence-aware interpretation performs}

\subsubsection*{Conditioning on prevalence resolves most metric discordance}

When practitioners condition on prevalence using the thresholds in Table~\ref{tab:thresholds}, $\kappa$, PABAK, and AC1 agree on classification in 98.4\% of scenarios (72 discordant out of 4,370). All 72 discordant scenarios occur in the prevalence-dominant regime. Under bias dominance and at balanced prevalence, the three metrics agree on classification in 100\% of scenarios. Discordance is concentrated at extreme prevalence with small sample sizes and asymmetric rater profiles where $\kappa$'s marginal-based chance model is unstable. Figure~\ref{fig:discordance} shows the discordance rate by prevalence and sample size.

\section*{Discussion}
%% ============================================================

Landis-Koch labels produce consistent interpretations at balanced prevalence but can assign different qualitative labels to identical raters at extreme prevalence. Practitioners working with rare conditions or highly prevalent findings should establish their prevalence before interpreting any agreement coefficient. In our simulation, label consistency was 100\% at balanced prevalence (1,330 of 1,330 scenarios) but dropped to 36.1\% at extreme prevalence (412 of 1,140).

\citet{cicchetti1990high} recommended reporting $P_{\text{pos}}$ and $P_{\text{neg}}$ alongside $\kappa$ to diagnose the paradox. GRASS builds on this recommendation by adding prevalence-conditioned thresholds and a decision procedure with explicit terminal outcomes. The closest structural precedent is the \citet{koo2016guideline} framework for intraclass correlation coefficients, which ties interpretation to study design and confidence intervals rather than fixed labels. GRASS extends this principle to categorical agreement, with prevalence as the conditioning variable.

Prevalence-invariant alternatives exist. \citet{hoehler2000bias} proposed an adjusted $\kappa$ based on the odds ratio, which \citet{walter2001hoehler} showed is equivalent to Yule's $Y$. Adjusted $\kappa$ does not vary with prevalence, but it is undefined when any cell count is zero and overestimates reliability when bias or prevalence effects are present. When all four cells are nonzero and the primary concern is comparing agreement across studies with different prevalence levels, adjusted $\kappa$ provides a useful prevalence-invariant benchmark. The goal of GRASS is not to propose a superior metric. It is to help practitioners interpret the metrics already in widespread use.

Since PABAK is prevalence-adjusted and bias-adjusted, a natural question is whether GRASS should also condition on rater bias. In our simulation, prevalence explains 15.3\% of scenario-level $\kappa$ variation and bias explains 17.2\%. These proportions reflect the grid composition (73.5\% prevalence-dominant, 22.8\% bias-dominant) and should not be interpreted as universal estimates of relative importance. The simulation grid includes 995 bias-dominant scenarios alongside 3,210 prevalence-dominant ones. Under bias dominance, all three metrics agree on classification in 100\% of scenarios when prevalence-conditioned thresholds are applied. All 72 discordant scenarios occur in the prevalence-dominant regime. The reason is structural: at balanced prevalence (where bias dominates), the three metrics converge and the thresholds are least sensitive to the chance model. Leave-one-profile-out cross-validation (Table~\ref{tab:jackknife}) confirms that no single profile drives the thresholds. Removing any one profile shifts the $\kappa$ threshold by at most 0.074.

\subsection*{Limitations}

The simulation assumes conditional independence of raters given the true class---that is, given the true status of a subject, one rater's decision does not influence the other's. Positively correlated errors would inflate $P_0$, PABAK, and AC1, which would make the GRASS thresholds too lenient. The grid covers binary 2-rater settings only; multi-class and multi-rater extensions are needed.

The $P_{\text{pos}} \geq 0.50$ threshold in Step 4 lacks the formal derivation that the Step 3 thresholds receive. Unlike the prevalence-conditioned thresholds, which are derived via ROC optimization, the 0.50 cutoff is a face-validity choice. Prevalence-conditioned $P_{\text{pos}}$ thresholds are a natural extension.

The ground-truth quality bands (High, Medium, Low) are defined by $\min(\text{Se}, \text{Sp})$ across both raters. Alternative definitions---such as mean accuracy, the Youden index of the weaker rater, or a cost-weighted loss function---would produce different thresholds. The sensitivity analysis in Appendix~\ref{app:stability} confirms that the qualitative conclusion holds under strict and lenient cutoffs, but the specific threshold values in Table~\ref{tab:thresholds} are conditional on the $\min(\text{Se}, \text{Sp})$ convention. The ROC optimization via Youden's $J$ assumes symmetric misclassification costs; practitioners in safety-critical domains where falsely certifying poor raters is more costly than conservatively flagging adequate ones may prefer more conservative thresholds.

\section{External Validation: HRPU Inter-Rater Reliability}
\label{app:hrpu}

To test the framework's performance on real data, we applied GRASS to a study of inter-rater reliability for the hand-release push-up (HRPU) event of the Army Combat Fitness Test. Seventeen raters independently scored 10 videos of HRPU performances across three test sessions for a total of 21,470 binary ratings (counted or not counted). Prevalence of counted repetitions ranged from 3.6\% to 93.3\% across videos and spans nearly the full prevalence spectrum.

We computed $\kappa$, PABAK, and AC1 for all 4,080 pairwise rater comparisons within each video and test session.

\subsection*{Before GRASS: metric choice determines the conclusion}

Without GRASS, even reporting all three metrics produces conflicting conclusions that the practitioner has no principled way to reconcile. Applying Landis-Koch labels and treating ``moderate'' or better as acceptable, $\kappa$ classified 27.6\% of comparisons as acceptable, PABAK classified 70.0\%, and AC1 classified 76.3\%. All three metrics received the same L-K label in only 34.2\% of comparisons. In 42.5\% of comparisons, $\kappa$ labeled the raters as unacceptable while PABAK labeled them as acceptable.

\subsection*{After GRASS: prevalence-conditioned thresholds reduce ambiguity}

With GRASS thresholds from Table~\ref{tab:thresholds}, all three metrics agreed on classification in 66.9\% of comparisons---nearly double the 34.2\% agreement rate under fixed labels. Of the 4,080 comparisons, 26.1\% exceeded all three prevalence-conditioned thresholds, 40.8\% fell below all three, and 33.1\% were discordant. The remaining discordance was concentrated at extreme prevalence (0.90--0.99), where $\kappa$ is most severely suppressed.

The critical discrepancy is between comparisons that fixed labels call acceptable but all three GRASS thresholds reject. Among the 442 comparisons (10.8\%) where PABAK's L-K label was ``moderate'' or better but all three GRASS thresholds indicated failure, 44.8\% had $P_{\text{neg}} = 0$ (zero agreement on negative cases) and 71.3\% had $P_{\text{neg}} < 0.50$. These rater pairs agree on the majority class but fail on the minority class---a distinction that fixed labels do not surface.

\subsection*{Worked example}

All 17 raters evaluated the same 50 repetitions from a video with an observed positive rate of 89\%. One pairwise comparison produced the following $2 \times 2$ table:

\noindent Without GRASS, the three metrics produce three different conclusions: $\kappa = -0.12$ (``poor''), $\text{PABAK} = 0.56$ (``moderate''), $\text{AC1} = 0.73$ (``substantial'').

\textbf{Step 1:} $P_0 = 0.78$, $P_{\text{pos}} = 0.88$, $P_{\text{neg}} = 0.00$, $\text{PI} = 0.78$, $\text{BI} = 0.02$. \textbf{Step 2:} Estimated prevalence is 0.89. \textbf{Step 3:} At prevalence 0.90, the thresholds are $\kappa \geq 0.377$, $\text{PABAK} \geq 0.640$, and $\text{AC1} \geq 0.733$. All three metrics fall below their thresholds. \textbf{Step 4:} $P_{\text{neg}} = 0.00$. The raters never agree on negative cases.

The ``moderate'' PABAK and ``substantial'' AC1 are artifacts of prevalence imbalance (PI $= 0.78$), not evidence of adequate agreement. The raters agree on counted repetitions but have zero agreement on not-counted repetitions.

\section{Algebraic Framework}
\label{app:algebra}

Consider two raters classifying $N$ subjects into binary categories. Their joint ratings produce a $2 \times 2$ table with cells $a$ (both 0), $b$ (R1=0, R2=1), $c$ (R1=1, R2=0), $d$ (both 1).

Observed agreement:
\begin{equation}
P_0 = \frac{a + d}{N}
\label{eq:p0}
\end{equation}

Cohen's $\kappa$ \citep{cohen1960coefficient}:
\begin{equation}
\kappa = \frac{P_0 - P_e}{1 - P_e}, \qquad P_e = \frac{(a+b)(a+c) + (c+d)(b+d)}{N^2}
\label{eq:kappa}
\end{equation}

PABAK \citep{byrt1993bias}:
\begin{equation}
\text{PABAK} = 2P_0 - 1
\label{eq:pabak}
\end{equation}

This is algebraically equivalent to $P_e = 0.5$. The Brennan-Prediger coefficient \citep{brennan1981coefficient} generalizes to $P_e = 1/K$.

Gwet's AC1 \citep{gwet2008computing}:
\begin{equation}
\text{AC1} = \frac{P_0 - 2\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{1 - 2\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}
\label{eq:ac1}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}$ is the average positive rate across raters. AC1's chance term shrinks when one category dominates, so AC1 is inflated relative to $\kappa$ at extreme prevalence.

\subsection*{The divergence between $\kappa$ and PABAK}

Since $P_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}(\text{PABAK} + 1)$, substituting into (\ref{eq:kappa}) gives:
\begin{equation}
\kappa = \frac{\text{PABAK} - \Delta}{1 - \Delta}, \qquad \Delta = 2P_e - 1
\label{eq:divergence}
\end{equation}

When $\Delta = 0$ (balanced marginals), $\kappa = \text{PABAK}$. As $\Delta \to 1$, $\kappa \to 0$ regardless of $P_0$.

\citet{byrt1993bias} decomposed $P_e$ using the prevalence index PI $= |d-a|/N$ and bias index BI $= |b-c|/N$:
\begin{equation}
P_e = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\text{PI}^2 - \text{BI}^2}{2}, \qquad \Delta = \text{PI}^2 - \text{BI}^2
\label{eq:delta_decomp}
\end{equation}

Prevalence imbalance ($\text{PI}^2$) inflates $P_e$ and attenuates $\kappa$. Rater bias ($\text{BI}^2$) acts in the opposite direction. In the typical low-prevalence study, PI is large, BI is small, $\Delta \approx \text{PI}^2$, and $\kappa$ can be severely attenuated even when $P_0$ is high.

AC1's chance term ($P_e^{\text{AC1}} = 2\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})$) does not decompose into PI and BI. It adapts to the overall category distribution. The result is inflation relative to $\kappa$ at extreme prevalence but overcorrection at intermediate prevalence.

Positive and negative agreement decompose agreement by class:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{pos}} = \frac{2d}{2d + b + c}, \qquad P_{\text{neg}} = \frac{2a}{2a + b + c}
\label{eq:posneg}
\end{equation}

\section{Simulation Design}
\label{app:sim}

Binary rating data were generated from a latent-class model. A true class $Y \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ was drawn for each subject. Two raters produced ratings conditional on $Y$ through sensitivity and specificity parameters, with conditional independence given $Y$.

\section{Confidence Interval Analysis}
\label{app:ci}

The standard Wald CI for $\kappa$ ($\kappa \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \hat{\sigma}_\kappa$) can exceed the $[-1,1]$ parameter bounds at small $N$ and extreme prevalence. We implement a logit-transformed alternative:
\begin{equation}
\kappa_{\text{logit}}^{\pm} = 2 \cdot \text{logit}^{-1}\!\left(\text{logit}(\kappa^*) \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\hat{\sigma}_\kappa}{2\kappa^*(1 - \kappa^*)}\right) - 1
\label{eq:logit_ci}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa^* = (\kappa + 1)/2$. This interval is guaranteed to lie within $[-1,1]$. The logit transform inherits the reliability of $\hat{\sigma}_\kappa$; for $N < 30$ at extreme prevalence, bootstrap CIs remain the gold standard.

A naive Wilson-type approach (Wilson score interval applied to $P_0$, transformed to the $\kappa$ scale via $(\cdot - P_e)/(1-P_e)$) produces \textit{worse} boundary violations than Wald (77.5\% at $N=10$, extreme prevalence) because the small denominator $1 - P_e$ amplifies the interval width.

\section{Prevalence Estimation from Rater Data}
\label{app:prevalence}

\textcolor{red}{Rachel: need your help here confirming this makes sense!}

When population prevalence is not known from the study design or an external source, it can be estimated from the raters' marginal positive rates. Each rater's positive rate is computed from the contingency table:
\begin{equation}
\hat{p}_1 = \frac{c + d}{N}, \qquad \hat{p}_2 = \frac{b + d}{N}
\end{equation}

The pooled estimate averages across both raters:
\begin{equation}
\hat{p} = \frac{\hat{p}_1 + \hat{p}_2}{2}, \qquad \text{SE}(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1 - \hat{p})}{N}}
\label{eq:prev_est}
\end{equation}

Report the point estimate with a 95\% confidence interval ($\hat{p} \pm 1.96 \cdot \text{SE}$). When the confidence interval spans multiple rows of Table~\ref{tab:thresholds}, check whether the metrics exceed their thresholds at both ends of the interval.

This pooled estimate assumes that both raters have similar marginal positive rates. The bias index (BI) from Step 1 quantifies this assumption: when BI is large, the raters have substantially different positive rates and the pooled estimate should be interpreted with caution. In that case, report both raters' individual positive rates alongside the pooled estimate.

Two notes on this estimate. First, the pooled marginal rate estimates the \textit{observed} positive rate, not the true prevalence. Imperfect sensitivity and specificity inflate or deflate the marginals relative to the true base rate. The estimate is adequate for selecting the correct row of the threshold table but should not be reported as a prevalence estimate without this caveat. Second, the standard error in (\ref{eq:prev_est}) uses $N$ (the number of subjects) in the denominator rather than $2N$ (the total number of ratings). Because both raters rate the same subjects, the ratings are clustered within subjects. Using $N$ conservatively treats the data as $N$ independent subject-level observations, which provides an upper bound on the standard error and prevents practitioners from falsely inflating their precision. The true variance lies between $\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})/2N$ (if raters were independent) and $\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})/N$ (if raters agreed perfectly), so this formula is conservative by construction. A more principled approach when both sensitivity and specificity are unknown is the latent-class estimator of \citet{hui1980estimating}, which jointly estimates prevalence and rater operating characteristics. The pooled marginal estimate used here is a practical approximation that avoids the complexity of latent-class modeling while remaining adequate for selecting the correct threshold row.

\subsection*{Worked example (continued)}

In the chest film example, if the 5\% population prevalence were unknown:
\begin{align*}
\hat{p}_1 &= 15/200 = 0.075 \\
\hat{p}_2 &= 13/200 = 0.065 \\
\hat{p} &= 0.070, \qquad \text{SE} = \sqrt{0.070 \times 0.930 / 200} = 0.018 \\
95\%\ \text{CI} &= [0.035,\ 0.105]
\end{align*}

\noindent The confidence interval spans the 0.05 and 0.10 rows of Table~\ref{tab:thresholds}. The $\kappa$ thresholds at these rows are 0.243 and 0.378. The observed $\kappa = 0.54$ exceeds both, so the conclusion is unchanged. The low BI (0.01) confirms that pooling is appropriate in this case.
